What are Linear Functions?

From the name itself, you can tell that a linear function produces a line. In the previous section, we looked at some examples of linear functions. But what does it mean to be linear?

Linear functions have a constant slope (one that doesn't change).

Let's look at the base function of a linear function.

This is produced by the equation `color(fuchsia)(y)=color(lawngreen)(x)`, which means that for every increase in `color(fuchsia)(y)`, there will be an equal increase in `color(lawngreen)(x)`, as both sides of the equation must be equal.

We can modify this base function to produce a line anywhere we want on the page.

Modifying the Slope

First, let's multiply `color(lawngreen)(x)` by the variable `color(cyan)(m)`.

If we change our new variable `color(cyan)(m)`, we can change the slope of the line, as we're multiplying `color(lawngreen)(x)` by `color(cyan)(m)` to produce `color(fuchsia)(y)`.

Move the m slider to change the slope of the line.

In our new equation `color(fuchsia)(y)=color(cyan)(m)color(lawngreen)(x)`, if `color(cyan)(m)` is positive (bigger than 0), the slope is positive, which means that as `color(lawngreen)(x)` increases, `color(fuchsia)(y)` increases. If `color(cyan)(m)` is negative (smaller than 0), the slope is negative, meaning that as `color(lawngreen)(x)` increases, `color(fuchsia)(y)` decreases.

As our slope (`color(cyan)(m)`) gets closer to 0, the angle the slope makes with the x-axis decreases. As `color(cyan)(m)`'s absolute value gets smaller, `color(lawngreen)(x)` will be multiplied by a smaller number, resulting in smaller `color(fuchsia)(y)` values.

If we look at this new line on a graph next to the old line, you'll notice that all the points have been translated up by 1 unit.

You may also notice that the number we added to `color(lawngreen)(x)`, is the same as the y-coordinate of the y-intercept, which is the point at which the line crosses the y-axis.

Let's call this new variable to change the y-intercept `color(yellow)(c)`. This gives us the equation:

`color(fuchsia)(y)=color(cyan)(m)color(lawngreen)(x)+color(yellow)(c)`

You may notice that this function looks similar to the one from the previous page, but now you know what `color(cyan)(m)` and `color(yellow)(c)` do!

Now, let's try some questions to apply our knowledge.

For the line `y=2x+7`, when `x=3`, what will `y` equal?

If we substitute `x=3` into the equation, we get `y=2(3)+7`, which simplifies to `y=13` (using our order of operations rules). Therefore, the point `(3, 13)` is on our graph.

`Car A` costs $4000, and $400 to maintain per month. `Car B` costs $6000, but costs $200 to maintain every month. After a year, which car will be cheaper?

Let's represent each car as an equation. If we think about time (the number of months) as the x-axis, then we can multiply the monthly cost by the number of months in a year to get the cost of the car for a year. Therefore, our monthly cost is the slope (`m`), and our number of months is `x`.

Our additional upfront cost can be added onto this number, so we can think of the upfront cost as our y-intercept (the cost for the car when `x=0`, or when the number of months is 0).

This gives us the equations `y=400x+4000` for Car A, and `y=200x+6000` for Car B.

If we substiute 12 (the number of months in a year) for `x` in each equation, we find that Car A costs `$9200`, and Car B costs `$8400`. Therefore, Car B is cheaper
