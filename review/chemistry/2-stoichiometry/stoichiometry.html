2.1: Moles and Molar Mass

Moles are an important unit of measurement in chemistry, that represents the amount of a substance. A mole is around `6.02*10^(23)` atoms of a substance. That number is known as Avogadro's number.

The molar mass of any element can be found on the top right of each element on your periodic table, expressed in grams per mole (`g/(mol)`). This can be used to find the molar mass. For example, say we are trying to find the how many moles are in 6g of Carbon:

`(6g C)/1 * (1mol)/(12.01g C) = 0.5 mol`

As you can see, the units cancel out and leave us with moles. Now, if we want to find the number of atoms in 0.5mol of Carbon, we can use Avogadro's number:

`(0.5mol)/1 * (6.02*10^(23))/(1 mol) = 3.01*10^(23) text(atoms)`

Check your understanding: How many atoms are in 12g of Magnesium?

We can use the molar mass of Magnesium (24.305g/mol) and Avogadro's number to calculate the number of atoms.

`(12g Mg)/1 * (1mol)/(24.305g) * (6.02*10^(23)text( atoms))/(1 mol)=2.97*10^(23)text( atoms of Mg)`

Check your understanding: How many grams does `3.01*10^(23)` atoms of `Li_2O` weigh?

We can use Avogadro's number to get to moles, and then use the molar mass of `Li_2O`, which is the molar mass of Li multiplied by 2, added to the molar mass of Oxygen. This is 29.881g/mol.

`(3.01*10^(23)text( atoms))/1 * (1mol)/(6.02*10^(23)text( atoms)) * (29.881g text( O))/(1mol) = 14.94g`
