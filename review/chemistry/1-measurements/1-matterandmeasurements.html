Matter and Measurements

This unit covers the basics of naming elements and compounds, balancing reactions, and measurements in the context of Chemistry, including important units and figures.

1.1: SI Units

SI units are a system of standardized units used across the sciences.

There are 7 base units that are most commonly used:

Mass Length Time Current Temperature Substance Amount Luminosity
Kilogram (kg) Meter (m) Seconds (s) Ampere (A) Kelvin (K) Mole(mol) Candela (cl)

Base units can be given prefixes in order to make it easier to work with smaller values. You can multiply these units by a factor of `10^n` determined by their prefix to get the value of it in SI units.

PrefixMultiplied by `10^n`
Giga (G)`10^9`
Mega (M)`10^6`
Kilo (k)`10^3`
Deca (da)`10^1`
Deci (d)`10^-1`
Centi (c)`10^-2`
Mili (m)`10^-3`
Micro (µ)`10^-6`
Nano (n)`10^-9`
Check your understanding: Convert `100g` to `kg`.

By looking at the prefix of the unit we are converting to (k), we know that we are looking for kilograms. We can use a conversion factor to find this, by cancelling out our orignial unit.

`(100g)/(1) * (1kg)/(10^3g) =0.1kg`

From these base units come derived units, which are units that are algebraically combined to create new units that usually tell us how many of one unit we have per other unit.

This is where conversion factors become important to solving questions. You must ensure when converting between units that the original unit is cancelled out when doing fractions, so you end up with your new unit. For example, if we are converting from meters per second to kilometers per hour:

`(10m)/(1s) * (1km)/(10^3m) * (3600s)/(1hr) = (36km)/(hr)`

Make sure that any unit you don't want is cancelled out, by having it appear the same amount of times on the numerator and denominator.

Check your understanding: A pure substance has a mass of 96g. Assuming it has a molar mass of 16g/mol, how many moles are in the substance?

We have a weight in grams, and a conversion factor that converts from grams to moles. We can rearrange this to make it easier to solve.

`(96g)/(1) * (1mol)/(16g) = 6mol`

The mol is written as the numerator in the conversion factor, so that the grams can cancel out, and only moles are left.

Important: Since some conversions may not be obvious, remember a mililiter (mL) is equal to a centimeter cubed (`cm^3`) and a liter (L) is equal to a decimeter cubed (`dm^3`).

Important: Also remember that temperature conversions work a little differently, and to convert from degrees Kelvin to degrees Celsius, you must subtract approx. 273 degrees.

1.3: Physical and Chemical Properties

Physical properties can be measured by observing them, and these do not involve any changes in bonding.

Chemical properties involve changes in bonds, as they can be measured in the way they react with other elements.

Check your understanding: Is malleability a physical or chemical property?

Malleability is the ability for a metal to be bent permanently without breaking. This does not result in changes in the bonds, therefore, we can classify this as a physical property.

1.4: Matter States and Classifications

The three main states of matter are solids, liquids, and gasses.

The processes of going between states are as follows below:

Solid `->` Melting `->` Liquid `->` Evaporation `->` Gas

Gas `->` Condensation `->` Liquid `->` Freezing `->` Solid

Solid `->` Sublimation `->` Gas `->` Deposition `->` Solid

Check your understanding: Water droplets form on a window during a cold day, while the indoor temperature is warm. What change of state occured?

Condensation occured as water vapour in the air cooled to form liquid droplets on the window.

Pure substances are 1 type of particle, while compounds are made of multiple types of particles in a specific ratio.

There are 2 types of mixtures: homogenous (uniformly mixed) and heterogeneous (not uniformly mixed). Mixures are substances that have not been chemically combined.

1.5: Naming and Balancing Ionic Compounds

Important: Ionic compounds are between a metal and a non-metal. These are located to the left and right of a periodic table, respectively.

In an ionic compound, you must ensure the charges of all elements add up to 0. For example, if you have Lithium and Oxygen, you first look at the charges:

`Li^(1+)` and `O^(2-)`

In order to balance them, we must get a net charge of `0` by using the least amount of each element as possible. If we have `2 Li` instead of `1 Li`, the charge is also multiplied by 2, giving us charges of `+2` and `-2`, which add to `0`. Therefore, `Li_2O` would be the ionic compound.

To name an ionic compound, you replace the ending with -ide of the second element. The metal is always written first in the name of an ionic compound, then the non-metal.

Check your understanding: Name the ionic compound that forms with Lithium and Oxygen.

An ionic compound with Lithium and Oxygen would be named Lithium Oxide, as the ending of the non-metal is removed and replaced with -ide.

Check your understanding: Find the name and formula of an ionic compound that forms between Nitrogen and Magnesium.

An ionic compound with Magnesium and Nitrogen would be named Mangesium Nitride, as the ending of the non-metal is removed and replaced with -ide. Remember that the metal always goes first, then the non metal.

To find its formula, remember, you want the charges to add up to 0. Mg has a charge of `2+` and N has a charge of `3-`. If we have 3 Mg and 2 N, we get net charges of `6+` and `6-`, which add to 0. Therefore, the formula is `Mg_3N_2`.

Important: Remember your polyatomic ions! These can form ionic compounds with other elements!
